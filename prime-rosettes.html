Prime rosettes

One rule, different prime-length cutoffs, same alignment.

Plain language
I’m running a repeating color order around each rosette. The order always starts with red at the top and moves one step at a time around the circle. The twist is that each rosette is only allowed to use a certain number of steps before the sequence “wraps” and starts again at red.

Each rosette uses a different prime number of steps (2, 3, 5, 7, 11, …). I’m watching how the overall rhythm changes when the “restart point” changes, even though the underlying rule stays the same.

The larger petals at 12, 2, 4, 6, 8, and 10 o’clock act like a quick readout: they show how the prime-length wrap interacts with the 6-hue rainbow cycle.
Systems view
This is a cyclic generator with a fixed phase origin (red at 12 o’clock) and a fixed advancement rule (advance one step per sector). Each rosette applies a different prime cutoff length, creating an interruption seam where the sequence resets.

A second layer is visible because hues repeat every 6 steps: projecting the long sequence onto the 6-hue “coarse cycle” makes mod 6-style interactions legible. Sampling the physically larger petals at six evenly spaced angles makes those interactions easy to compare across different prime cutoffs.
Formal view (optional)
Let sectors be indexed by k = 0,1,2,… starting at 12 o’clock, advancing clockwise. For a chosen modulus p (prime), the rosette uses the cycle k mod p to select the k-th color in an ordered list, with a fixed phase origin at k=0.

Hue classes correspond to the projection k mod 6 (six rainbow hues). The “large petal” readout samples six fixed angular positions, i.e. a fixed subset of indices, producing a compact signature of how the mod-p wrap interacts with the mod-6 projection.
